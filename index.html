1894 – 1895

The Sino-Japanese War

Korea becomes the object — never the subject — of great power competition. China and Japan fight over the peninsula. Korea does not choose this war. Korea does not benefit from it.

Georges Bigot, "Une partie de pêche" (1887) · Korea is the fish.

1894

1904 – 1905

The Russo-Japanese War

Same pattern. Different empire. Russia replaces China. Japan defeats a European power for the first time in modern history. The West is stunned. Korea pays the price.

Rostro, "Korea crushed between Japan and Russia" (1904) · Public Domain

1904

1950 – 1953

The Korean War

A civil war becomes a Cold War proxy. The division — imposed from outside at the 38th parallel — becomes permanent. Not because of ideology. Because of geography and timing.

Armistice signing, Panmunjom, July 27, 1953 · South Korea was not a signatory.

1950

Getting North Korea Wrong

This Is Not
a Failed State

Nuclear weapons. A space program. A million-man army. Cyber capabilities that hit the global banking system. Flood recovery in two years that most countries couldn't match.

The satellite image says "failed." The DPRK hits way above its weight on every standard measure.

Mirae (Future) Scientists Street, Pyongyang

Getting South Korea Wrong

The Country Nobody
Sees Coming

Americans think they saved a small, grateful ally in 1950. They have no idea what that ally became.

The Fact That Breaks the Narrative

North Korea Was Richer
Until 1974

1960: North Korea's GDP per capita was 2.2x the South's. For a full generation after the war, the "communist" side was outperforming the "capitalist" side.

The crossover didn't happen until 1974. The satellite image captures the result. It hides the mechanism.

1974

Getting Ourselves Wrong

"Who Lost China?"

The Cold War framework that produced the satellite image narrative began with a question that was wrong from the start. China was never America's to lose. Korea was never a scoreboard.

Getting the Future Wrong

The 21st Century
Is Asia

China. India. Vietnam. Japan. Russia. And a unified Korea — 80 million people, $6 trillion in untapped minerals, the world's largest rare earth deposit, and South Korean technology.

Goldman Sachs projected a unified Korea surpassing France, Germany, and possibly Japan.

The satellite image makes you stop thinking about Korea. That is exactly what you cannot afford to do.
